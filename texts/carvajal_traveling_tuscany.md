<h2>Introduction to the Text</h2>

<p>This poem is a <i> serranilla</i>, an evolution of the Provençal <i> pastorela</i>. Written in short verse (<i> arte menor</i>), <i> serranillas </i> narrate a courtly poet’s encounter with a mountain woman. This is one of six compositions in the genre by fifteenth-century author Carvajal (or Carvajales). Very little is known about Carvajal’s life. His poetry is linked to the Neapolitan court of Alfonso the Magnanimous in Naples (r. 1442-1458) and to that of Alfonso’s son Ferrante (r. 1459-1494). In addition to his famous <i> serranillas</i>, Carvajal is also known for his literary epistles and ballads.</p>

<p>In this poem, the poet subverts some of the genre’s conventions by meeting not a rustic woman but a beautiful and courteous noble lady in a context in which he would have normally met a <i> serrana</i>. The two of them strike up a conversation as they walk together. The end of the composition reveals that the encounter was a dream vision.</p>

<h2>Introduction to the Source</h2>
<p>The poem is copied in Madrid, Biblioteca Nacional de España, VITR/17/7, fol. 136v-137r. This manuscript is a copy of the poetry collection known as the <i> Cancionero de Estúñiga</i> <a href="http://bdh-rd.bne.es/view-" target="_blank">, ca. 1465. It has been digitized: </a> http://bdh-rd.bne.es/viewer.vm?id=0000051837. It contains a compilation of mostly Castilian poems, including ballads, as well as a few Italian compositions. Their authors accompanied the King of Aragon, Alfonso the Magnanimous, in Naples in the mid-fifteenth century.</p>

<h2>Traveling through Tuscany | Passando por la Toscana</h2>
<p>Passando por la Toscana,</p>
<p>entre Sena et Florencia,</p>
<p>vi dama gentil galana</p>
<p>digna de grand reuerencia.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>5 Cara tenia de romana,</p>
<p>tocadura purtoguesa,</p>
<p>el ayre de castellana,</p>
<p>vestida como senesa</p>
<p>discretamente, non uana;</p>
<p>10 yo le fise reuerencia</p>
<p>y ella, con mucha prudencia,</p>
<p>bien mostro ser cortesana.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>Assy entramos por Sena,</p>
<p>fablando de compannia,</p>
<p>15 con plaser auiendo pena</p>
<p>del pesar que me plasia.</p>
<p>Sy se dilatara el dia</p>
<p>o la noche nos tomara,</p>
<p>tan grand fuego se encendia</p>
<p>20 que toda la tierra quemara.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>Vestia de blancho domasquino,</p>
<p>camurra al touillo cortada,</p>
<p>ençima de un uelud fino</p>
<p>un luto la falda rastrada;</p>
<p>25 ponposa et agraciada,</p>
<p>una inuencion traya</p>
<p>por letras que non entendia,</p>
<p>de perlas la manga bordada.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>Item mas, traya un ioyel</p>
<p>30 de richas piedras pesantes,</p>
<p>un balax y entorno d’el</p>
<p>çafis, rubis et dyamantes</p>
<p>firmando sobre la fruente</p>
<p>con muy grande resplandor,</p>
<p>35 pero dauale el fauor</p>
<p>Su gesto lyndo, plasiente.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>En su fabla, uestir et ser</p>
<p>non mostraua ser de mandra.</p>
<p>Queriendo su nonbre saber,</p>
<p>40 respondiome que Casandra.</p>
<p>Yo con tal nonbre oyr</p>
<p>muy alegre desperte</p>
<p>e tan solo me falle</p>
<p>que por Dios pense morir.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<b>Traveling through Tuscany,</b>
<p>between Siena and Florence,</p>
<p>I saw an elegant gentle lady</p>
<p>Worthy of great reverence.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>5 She had the face of a Roman,</p>
<p>A Portuguese headdress,</p>
<p>And air of a Castilian,</p>
<p>Was dressed like a Sienese,</p>
<p>Discreetly, not vain;</p>
<p>10 I bowed to her</p>
<p>And she, very cautiously,</p>
<p>Did prove to be courteous.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>That is how we entered Siena</p>
<p>Conversing in company</p>
<p>15 With pleasure and sorrow</p>
<p>For a grief that pleased me.</p>
<p>If the day had been extended</p>
<p>Or the night had arrived,</p>
<p>Such great fire would have been ignited</p>
<p>20 That it would have burned the earth out.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>Dressed in white damask,</p>
<p>Gamurra cut at the ankle</p>
<p>Over a fine velvet,</p>
<p>A mourning skirt down to the floor;</p>
<p>25 Pompous and graceful,</p>
<p>A verse she carried with her</p>
<p>With letters she did not understand,</p>
<p>On the sleeves embroidered with pearls.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>In addition, she wore a little jewel</p>
<p>30 Of rich heavy stones,</p>
<p>A purple spinel and, around it,</p>
<p>Sapphires, rubies, and diamonds</p>
<p>Fixed on her forehead</p>
<p>With such a great glow;</p>
<p>35 But what favored her the most</p>
<p>Was her pleasant, beautiful countenance.</p>
<p><br/></p>

<p>In her speech, dress, and demeanor</p>
<p>She did not appear to be rustic,</p>
<p>Wanting to know her name</p>
<p>40 She told me it was Casandra.</p>
<p>At just hearing such a name,</p>
<p>I woke up much delighted.</p>
<p>And I found myself so lonely</p>
<p>That by god I thought I would die.</p>
<p><br/></p>
