<h2>Introduction to the Text</h2>
<p>The Gosling is a typical example of the Middle High German tradition of mären: short narrative texts written in rhyming couplets, often telling humorous stories spiked with sexual jokes and sideswipes against specific social groups, most frequently clerics. In this way, mären are similar to the French fabliaux and the Italian prose novelle.</p>

<p>The Gosling was probably composed in the second half of the thirteenth century CE, and its author is unknown. The text tells the story of a young monk who leaves his monastery for the first time, encountering an outside world about which he is entirely ignorant. Never having seen a woman before, he innocently asks his abbot about those that they meet, and the abbot, trying to suppress the monk’s instant fascination, tells him that these creatures are called “geese”. The abbot’s plan fails as the naive monk is soon seduced by a village girl. Unaware of his wrongdoing, he reveals his sexual encounter to the abbot, who recognizes the calamity caused by his white lie.</p>

<p>The motif of the young man inexperienced in the ways of the world is widespread in European medieval literature. It is central to the various medieval versions of the legend of Barlaam and Josaphat (for example, Rudolf von Ems’s <i>Barlaam und Josaphat</i>, also in Middle High German, composed c.1220/30) and is frequently used as a moral anecdote in sermons and exemplary literature (for example, Jacques de Vitry’s Exempla, no. 82). Although commonly found in texts intended to educate their listener, the motif’s humorous potential did not escape medieval authors and audiences, and there are a large number of burlesque medieval stories about young men who have never seen women before and yet are instantly attracted to them. For example, both the early fourteenth-century Italian text <i>Novellino </i>(specifically story no. 14) and Giovanni Boccaccio’s <i>Decameron </i>(composed in 1358; see specifically the introduction to the fourth day) tell variants of this story.</p>

<p>The Gosling is a relatively early example of this narrative tradition. With regard to its verse form, sexual humor and undertones of anticlericalism, it represents central characteristics of the mären tradition and provides an introduction this literary genre.</p>

<h2>Introduction to the Source</h2>
<p>Multiple variants of this text are transmitted in six manuscripts that were written between the early fourteenth and mid-fifteenth centuries CE. The manuscript that our translation is based on was composed in Würzberg (in what is now southern Germany) around the year 1350 CE. In the scholarship, it is known as manuscript E.</p>

<h2>About this Edition</h2>

<p>I heard a story told of a splendid</p>
<p>and well-built monastery, as a monastery should be.</p>
<p>5 Their lodgings and their infirmary did not have limited meal times for whenever someone arrived on horseback or on foot</p>
<p>he always found a meal prepared.</p>
<p>10 Charitably and willingly</p>
<p>they gave whatever they had.1</p>
<p>Would that monasteries were still this way! I also heard something more about them: that their monastery was isolated</p>
<p>15 so that strangers seldom saw the monks and their quarters. The same story also tells that there were many monks</p>
<p>that seldom left the monastery.</p>
<p>20 As it happened, a young man lived there. He had spent all his years there.</p>
<p>since he was a child,</p>
<p>staying inside the monastery. He couldn’t have known</p>
<p>25 what dwelled in the land.</p>
<p>He had only heard tell of horses: that one could ride them.</p>
<p>There came a time</p>
<p>when the abbot needed to ride out.</p>
<p>30 Without delay,</p>
<p>he wanted to attend to the monastery’s affairs.</p>
<p>The same lad asked him</p>
<p>to take him along through the countryside so that he would learn about</p>
<p>35 the land’s customs</p>
<p>of which he knew very little. The abbot granted</p>
<p>the monk what he desired</p>
<p>as he saw that he was ignorant.</p>
<p>40 That transpired for this reason: he thought: “Should the lad learn about both land and people,</p>
<p>one could trust him with</p>
<p>the duties that need to be fulfilled.</p>
<p>45 Then he will become a very useful man for us.” With that he led him from there.</p>
<p>His pages didn’t hesitate to ride with their master.</p>
<p>Their horses cantered nicely along.</p>
<p>50 Once they reached the countryside, the monk never neglected to ask about whatever animal crossed his path. “What’s that called?” he would ask. The abbot readily told him</p>
<p>55 what its name was.</p>
<p>Whether cattle or sheep or swine, he informed him correctly.</p>
<p>After a short while they came</p>
<p>to a farmstead to which they were headed</p>
<p>60 and intended to stay. When the reeve saw them,</p>
<p>he approached them and said:</p>
<p>“God bid you welcome, my dear Sir, and all those here with you.”</p>
<p>65 After the horses had been taken care of, the abbot and the monks went</p>
<p>to rest by the fire. Right away,</p>
<p>someone promptly took</p>
<p>70 their shoes and coats.</p>
<p>Now, the host had a beautiful wife and a daughter whose body</p>
<p>was everything one could wish for. She was about twenty years old.</p>
<p>75 The two women also came there and welcomed the gentlemen. The abbot asked them to be seated.</p>
<p>They were not averse to this. They sat down on the spot. But the monk asked the abbot to tell him</p>
<p>what these creatures were called. The abbot replied readily:</p>
<p>“These are called geese.”</p>
<p>The monk said: “My goodness! Geese are lovely.</p>
<p>Why don’t we have geese?</p>
<p>They would fit in nicely</p>
<p>on the pasture at the monastery.” Both the host’s wife and daughter laughed at that.</p>
<p>They were very surprised that he was so handsome,</p>
<p>yet didn’t know</p>
<p>what a woman was called.</p>
<p>Straight away, they asked the abbot</p>
<p>whether the gentleman was right in the head.</p>
<p>Then he told them the story that you have just heard</p>
<p>about how the monk had come to the monastery2 and grown up there.</p>
<p>When the host’s daughter had listened to this story,</p>
<p>she immediately thought:</p>
<p>”He’s such a gorgeous man. Truly, if I can make it happen</p>
<p>I will test whether he knows women in name and in the flesh.”</p>
<p>She kept her musing to herself; she told no one of the thoughts that she had formed.</p>
<p>It was now so late</p>
<p>that it was time for the gentlemen to go to bed.</p>
<p>The reeve now insisted</p>
<p>that beds be prepared for them as was befitting. His daughter was also present.</p>
<p>She arranged it so that the young man had his bed</p>
<p>far from the others so that his master</p>
<p>could be comfortable.</p>
<p>It was done according to his wishes.</p>
<p>Right after the gentlemen had been shown to their beds the host commanded</p>
<p>that all of his household should go to sleep</p>
<p>so that the gentlemen would be left in piece and quiet. The monk was not able to sleep.</p>
<p>He had many thoughts</p>
<p>about what each thing was called that he had encountered that day.</p>
<p>She also lay awake turning over in her mind how to accomplish</p>
<p>what she had thought of earlier.</p>
<p>After all the people around them had fallen asleep, she got up without a sound</p>
<p>and snuck over to his bed. When the monk noticed her,</p>
<p>he said straight away, “What might that be?” She said: “It’s me, the little gosling.</p>
<p>I’ve been suffering dreadfully from the cold. Sir, I would really like to ask you,</p>
<p>to permit me to slip under the covers out of the kindness of your heart,</p>
<p>so that I don’t freeze to death.”</p>
<p>The monk was so naive</p>
<p>that he let her slip under the covers. That was done out of kindness.</p>
<p>When she joined him under the blanket, this young man knew</p>
<p>next to nothing</p>
<p>about how to play so-called bed games with her. She knew a little bit more.</p>
<p>With great skill she quickly got him</p>
<p>playing the same game.</p>
<p>The monk dealt with the goose eagerly, because it seemed to him</p>
<p>that he was feeling good and would soon feel even better.</p>
<p>He carried on with it</p>
<p>until she noticed the break of day. Then she got up and said:</p>
<p>“You must never tell anyone</p>
<p>what has happened between the two of us. If the abbot ever found out,</p>
<p>we would both immediately suffer a gruesome death.”</p>
<p>With great urgency she commanded that he never tell anyone about it.</p>
<p>He swore to that and she then retired to her chamber.</p>
<p>She was full of joy</p>
<p>that she had gone there and back without anyone noticing.</p>
<p>Soon after</p>
<p>she came to her chamber, the day began to dawn.</p>
<p>The abbot and the monk</p>
<p>did not stay in bed much longer.</p>
<p>They dealt with the affairs</p>
<p>for which they had come there. As soon as they had done this they rode home again.</p>
<p>Once they had arrived home, the brothers could not resist taking the monk aside</p>
<p>and eagerly asking him</p>
<p>how he had liked the country.</p>
<p>He began to tell them that he had actually seen</p>
<p>many things in the country that he hadn’t known before. They laughed a lot at that.</p>
<p>His tale entertained them greatly.</p>

<p>The monk did as he was ordered, but I think he was treated unfairly.</p>
<p>For, whatever sins he committed there, the abbot was to blame for them.</p>
<p>If he had told him the truth honestly and without lies,</p>
<p>the monk would have guarded himself better. Mockery and lying are seldom good;</p>
<p>they are sins and they are also without honour.</p>
<p>What more shall I say about this than what I have already said?</p>
<p>It is my belief and my assumption that in Swabia there might still easily be two or three monks</p>
