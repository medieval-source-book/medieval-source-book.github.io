<h2>Introduction to the Text</h2>
<p>The <i>Roman des Franceis</i>, also known as “Arflet,” is a short satirical piece in octosyllabic verses, arranged in four-line strophes. Its author, who names himself André (361, 371, 385, 395), has been confirmed by Gaston Paris to be André de Coutances, the Norman who is known as the author of an Évangile de Nicodème (Paris and Bos 1885, xvi–xix, xxiv–xxvii). It should be noted, however, that the attribution is hypothetical. Given its subject matter and the views expressed therein, it has been suggested that the <i>Roman des Franceis </i>could not have been written after 1204, when Philippe-Auguste invaded Normandy.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>The <i>Roman des Franceis </i>is presented as a charter issued by Alfred, king of Northumberland. It takes a biting stance towards the French, who are depicted as cowardly, stingy, and poor garlic-eating wretches. First, it stages the invasion of France by King Arthur (41-224), during which Arthur defeats Frollo, king of the French, in a duel (173-192) and throws the French into serfdom after the latter’s defeat (193-208). André then goes on to describe the stinginess of the French: to avoid petty fighting over food, they developed the tradition of tying pieces of bread to strings, allowing them to retrieve their share of soup (237-312; this motif has been studied by Félix Lecoy [1970]), and they have their guests pay for their share of food (345-360). The <i>Roman des Franceis </i>also includes a series of burlesque commandments proffered by Frollo (137-156). While the text chiefly targets the French, the English get their share of satirical observations too, heavy drinkers as they are (5-15). It should be noted that when the text refers to “the French,” it might be refering to the inhabitants of the small royal domain surrounding Paris (see 62-64). At the time it was likely written, half of what we know as France was under Plantagenet rule, while the other half was only nominally subject to the king of France. The list of names at the end of the poem (386-396), as well as the list of peers of France, copied in Latin after the <i>Roman </i>and transcribed here, attests to this.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<h2>Introduction to the Source</h2>
<p>The <i>Roman des Franceis </i>appears in London, British Library, MS Add. 10289, fols. 129v-132v. The manuscript was likely made in Mont-Saint-Michel in the late 13th century (Paris and Bos 1885, xix–xxiv; see also the BL manuscript notice), and besides the <i>Roman des Franceis</i>, it contains a number of other texts, listed by Paris and Bos. Some texts have a connection to the manuscript’s place of production, such as ours and Guillaume de Saint-Pair’s <i>Roman du Mont-Saint-Michel</i>, while others are less place-specific. The text of the <i>Roman des Franceis </i>is at times rather obscure, in all likelihood due to the vagaries of transmission. It has been edited by Achille Jubinal (1842, vol 2, 1–17), and more recently and with substantial improvements by Anthony Holden (1973). The GMS translation is based on an interpretive transcription of the <i>Roman des Franceis</i>, and occasionally draws on Holden’s edition, commentary or glossary to establish a legible text, where necessary.</p>

<h2>Romance of the French (Alfred) | Le Roman des Franceis (Arflet)</h2>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Ici se comence le Romanz des Franceis</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Reis Arflet de Nohundrelande As boens beveors saluz mande,</p>
<p>Les autres a maufez commande, Quer il nes prise une alemande.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Le viel Arflet fu son ancestre Qui des guides fu sire et mestre. Cil si enbat destre et senestre</p>
<p>Tant qu’il en set quanqu’en puet estre.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Mestre est des cerveises estales, Bien les conoist bones et males; Bien est sire des escales*,</p>
<p>Des gestinges et des ervales.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>De lui vos di en dreite fei Que sevent a beü sanz sei,</p>
<p>Et bien est veir que endreit sei Ne veut que dechie guersei.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Rimé ont de lui li Franceis Lez le pot ou bollent VI peis; Par icestes meismes leis S’en volent vengier li Engleis.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Il ont dit que riens n’a valu, Et donc à Arflet n’a chalu Que boté fu par Capalu</p>
<p>Li reis Artur en la palu;</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>25Et que le chat l’ocist de guerre, Puis passa outre en Engleterre, Et ne fu pas lenz de conquerre; Ainz porta corone en la terre,</p>
<p>Here begins the romance of the French</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>King Alfred of Northumberland Sends greetings to the good drinkers, The others he commends to demons,</p>
<p>Because he does not value them much*.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>5 Old Alfred was his ancestor,</p>
<p>He was the lord and master of banquets.</p>
<p>The other one throws himself from one side to the other So much that he knows all about it.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>He is the master of settled beer,</p>
<p>10 He knows it good and bad;</p>
<p>He is the lord of drinking bowls, Of banquets and of feasts.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>I tell you truthfully</p>
<p>That he has often drank without thirst,</p>
<p>15 And it is true that he does not want Wassail to fall into disuse.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>The French have made some rhymes about him, Next to a pot where six peas were boiling;</p>
<p>By the same token, the English</p>
<p>20 Want to take revenge.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>The English said that it has not worked out, And so Alfred did not care</p>
<p>That king Arthur was thrown</p>
<p>Into the marsh by the cat Capalu;</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>25 And that the cat killed him at war,</p>
<p>And crossed the Channel into England, And was quick to make conquests,</p>
<p>So that he even wore a crown in those lands,</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Et fu sire de la contree. Ou ont itel fable trovee?</p>
<p>Mençonge est, Dex le set, provee: Onc greignor ne fu encontree.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Mes li chaitif, li espové, Li mal peü, li tart cové, Li patarin, li endové,</p>
<p>Où ont-il d’autre contrové?</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Trop ont dit d’Artur grant enfance, Quer Artu fu de tel puissance</p>
<p>Que Franceis conquist o sa lance: Mau eritage mist en France.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Bien savon que Bien et Belin, Maximien et Costentin Furent a Franceis mal veisin,</p>
<p>Et France orent, ce est* la fin.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>D’Engleterre furent tuit rei,</p>
<p>Chescun conquist France endroit sei, Chescun en pleis a le bofei,</p>
<p>Le gorgeïr et le desrei.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Au rei Artur le deraain,</p>
<p>De celui sommes nos certain, Voudrent* fere plet, mes en vain, Quer il les out bien sor* sa main.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Quant de lor orguil s’averti, Maugré eus toz les converti, Et le païs acuverti:</p>
<p>Dites se ce est veir parti?</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Jamés n’iert jor que il n’i pere; Douce esteit* France, or est amere; Mout ourent en Artur dur pere,</p>
<p>Sa sorvenue mout compere.</p>
<p>And became the lord of the region.</p>
<p>30 Where did they find such a tale? It’s a proven lie, God knows it:</p>
<p>Never has one found a bigger one.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>But the miserable, the overwhelmed, The malnourished, the misbred,</p>
<p>35 The naughty, the possessed,</p>
<p>Where have they found these imaginary things?</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>They told enough silly tales about Arthur, Because Arthur was of such power</p>
<p>That he conquered the French with his lance:</p>
<p>40 He placed a wicked lineage in France.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>We know well that Brenne and Belin, Maximien and Constantin</p>
<p>Were bad neighbours to the French,</p>
<p>And they had France in their power, that’s how it ends.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>45 They were all kings of England,</p>
<p>Each one of them conquered France,</p>
<p>Each one was full of pride when he pleaded, Full of garrulousness and of reckless conduct.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>To Arthur, the last king,</p>
<p>50 —of him we are assured—</p>
<p>They wanted to contest his power, but that was in vain, Because he had them firmly in his command.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>When he noticed their arrogance, He turned them despite themselves,</p>
<p>55 And he subjected the land:</p>
<p>Tell me if that is a truthful distribution!</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>There was not a day that he would not be seen; France used to be sweet, now it is bitter;</p>
<p>They had a very harsh father in Arthur,</p>
<p>60 His arrival made a notable difference.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Mout fu Artur proz et corteis;</p>
<p>Quant out conquis Chartres et Bleis, Et Orliens et tot Estampeis,</p>
<p>A Paris vint o ses Engleis.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>La vile asist, n’en dotez mie; Mout out bone chevalerie</p>
<p>Et bien estruite et bien garnie, Si l’a fierement asallie.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Engleis fierement asallirent, Franceis merdement defendirent: Au premier assaut se rendirent, Et hontosement s’en partirent.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>A cel partir fu apelee Paris, ci n’a nul celee,</p>
<p>Qui primes fu Termes nommee, Et mout ert de grant renommee.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frolles ert apelé le reis, Qu’Artur conquist o ses Engleis; Et de Frolles sont dit Franceis: Qui primes eurent non Bailleis.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frolles, qui de France fu sire, Ne sout que faire ne que dire; Grant mautalent out et grant ire: Franceis manda a un concire.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Li baron l’ont a ce amis</p>
<p>Qui ses messages a tramis A Artur; si li a pramis</p>
<p>Qu’encor porroient estre amis,</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Se de sa terre s’en issist</p>
<p>Que a mout grant tort saisseïst; Et s’il ne la guerpisseïst</p>
<p>De batalle le aasteïst</p>
<p>Arthur was very worthy and courtly; Once he conquered Chartres and Blois, And Orléans and all of Étampes,</p>
<p>He went to Paris with his Englishmen.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>65 He besieged the city, let there be no doubt about it; He had very good knights,</p>
<p>Well trained and well-armed, So he bravely assailed it.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>The English bravely assailed,</p>
<p>70 The French shittily defended:</p>
<p>With the first assault they surrendered, And they went off full of shame.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>From this departure came the name Paris, there’s nothing to hide about it,</p>
<p>75 Which was initially called Termes, And which had a great renown.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frollo was the name of the king</p>
<p>Whom Arthur vanquished with his English; And the French are named after Frollo:</p>
<p>80 Initially, they were called Bailleis*.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frollo, who was the lord of France, Did not know what to do or to say; He was very angry and upset:</p>
<p>He convocated the French to a council.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>85 The barons persuaded him To send a messenger</p>
<p>To Arthur; and so he promised him That they could still be in good terms,</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>If he emptied the lands</p>
<p>90 Which he had been ill advised to seize; But if he did not vacate the premises, He would provoke him to combat,</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Par eus dous, que plus n’i eüst: Eissi le voleit, ce seüst.</p>
<p>Cous rendist qui cous receüst, Et plus feïst qui plus peüst.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Artur respondi: ‘Dex i valle; Defendré mei, s’est qui m’asalle; Seit a demein ceste batalle,</p>
<p>A Paris, en l’Isle, sanz falle.’</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>De ça et de la sunt certain De la batalle a l’endemain: Qui veintra tot, eit en sa main Les bois, les viles et le plain.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frolles durement menaça, De jurer ne s’apereça,</p>
<p>Dex tot par menbres depeça, Que Artur mal s’i aproça.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Artur, qui n’out pas cuer de glace, Preisa mout petit sa menace; Mieuz l’amast a tenir en place Que voer Dieu en mi la face.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Artur, qui out grant desierrer, Se fist matin aparellier,</p>
<p>Lui et Labagu son destrier, Et se fist en l’Isle nagier.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frolles jusqu’a tierce dormi, Et lors quant il se desdormi Endeseetes s’estormi</p>
<p>Com se l’eüssent point formi.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Franceis, qui moroient d’ennui, Li distrent: ‘Leverez-vos hui?’ Il dist ‘aol!’*, et de nullui</p>
<p>N’ont Franceis aol fors de lui.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>95</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>100</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>105</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>110</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>115</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>120</p>
<p>One on one, and he wouldn’t add anything to it:</p>
<p>He made it known to him that those were his terms. A blow was worth a blow,</p>
<p>And who could do more should do more.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Arthur replied: ‘God help me;</p>
<p>I will defend myself, if there’s someone to attack me; Let the combat be scheduled for tomorrow,</p>
<p>In Paris, on the Île de la Cité, without fail.’</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>On this and on that side, they are assured That they will fight the next day:</p>
<p>The winner would have possession</p>
<p>Of the forests, the towns and the clear lands.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frollo proffered strong menaces, He did not tire of swearing,</p>
<p>He completely dismembered the body of God, Because Arthur came to do ill.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Arthur, who did not have a cold heart, Cared very little about his threats;</p>
<p>He would have preferred him to stay in place Rather than seeing God face to face.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Arthur, who felt a great urge,</p>
<p>Had himself prepared early morning, Himself and his horse Labagu,</p>
<p>And he had himself transported to the Île.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>Frollo slept until way after daybreak, And when he finished sleeping,</p>
<p>He abruptly rose,</p>
<p>As if ants had bitten him.</p>
<p><br/></p>
<p>The French, who were bored to death, Asked him: ‘Will you get up today?’ He said ‘yeah’, and the French</p>
<p>Use garlic because of no one else than him.</p>
